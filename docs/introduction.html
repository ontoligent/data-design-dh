Introduction

The data science body of knowledge (DSBOK) may be divided into five broad areas: analytics, systems, design, value, and practice. The table below gives a sample of activities associated with each area.

The Five Areas of Data Science
AREA ACTIVITY
Analytics Statistical modeling, algorithmic modeling, simulations, pattern discovery, data reduction, etc.
Systems Programming, software development, ML ops, cloud computing, high-performance computing, etc.
Design Data modeling for databases, ontology making, visualization, data storytelling, etc.
Value Ethics, scientific research formulation, business value framing, strategic planning, etc.
Practice Project management, cross-functional team building, information architecture, connection to domain knowledge, etc.

These areas are elements of an invariant model derived from the image of the data processing pipeline that has dominated discourse about the definition of data science since its beginnings in the 1950s. This model is called the 4+1 model of data science. For a theoretical justification and brief historical overview of this model, see (Alvarado 2022). For purposes of this essay, it is helpful to recall that the data science pipeline may be thought of as an arc, in which the pre- and post-processing segments mirror each other and combine to form structural areas (see Appendix).

Design

Among the five areas of data science, the least well understood is design. This area encompasses the knowledge shared by both data modeling for databases (which belongs to the pre-processing segment of the pipeline) and visualization (which belongs to the post-processing segment). Design is most closely associated with the field of human-computer interaction (HCI), but also the fields of data modeling for databases and ontology design, which tend to be associated with with database and information management (which belong to systems). Because of these disparate associations, design tends not to be recognized as a separate field. One result of this lack of recognition is the under-theorization of the area.

To understand design on its own terms, consider the communicative relationship between humans and computational machines and how this relationship maps onto the pipeline of data science. On the pre-processing side, the work of data science is focused on converting information as captured and understood by humans — for example, in textual or analog form — into forms that can be processed by machines. (We call machine processable information data; more on this below.) On the post-processing side, data science is focused on converting the results of esoteric modeling and analysis into more humanly intelligible forms through the work of visualization and digital storytelling.

Formally, we may represent these moments as \(H \rightarrow M\) and \(M \rightarrow H\) (or \(H \leftarrow M\)). The following table describes some of the artifacts and practices currently associated with each:

Two Moments of Design
Pre-processing (\(H \rightarrow M\)) Post-processing (\(M \rightarrow H\))

Visual modeling: E-R diagrams, UML diagrams, etc.

Verbal classification: taxonomies and ontologies (e.g. RDF, SKOS, OWL), Knowledge Representation, etc.

Domain-specific models: protein models, text models, etc.

Interactive visualization: Dashboards, user-facing databases, etc.

Static visualization: infographics, posters, embedded figures and charts, etc.

Digital storytelling: integration of visual and verbal elements into narrative, cinematic, or immersive form, etc.

Data product design: user interface and interaction design of data-driven software products for end-users, etc.

What is common to these activities? In both moments, the knowledge worker develops and applies a formal language, verbal or visual, to create a functional representation that mediates between cognitive structures of humans and those of machines.1 These representations are of two kinds. The first kind are representations of the world, as understood by humans, to the computational and mathematical machinery that will process the represented information. The second kind are representations of the results of mathematical modeling and discovery to the world, i.e. to non-specialist stakeholders and to users of data products. Common to both cases are representations that mediate between the strict demands of mechanical processing and the more labile requirements of human cognition.

Given the focus of design on representation, what are some specific characteristics of representation in this context? A full answer to this question is not possible here for two reasons. First, the topic itself is broad and will eventually require volumes for a systematic treatment, as we can expect with any well-developed idea within a body of knowledge. Second, and as noted, the topic is under-theorized. Although some important contributions have been made to the field, especially from the field of HCI, it remains understudied, especially in the uniform manner implied by the 4+1 model of data science.

Here we may note the following features shared by both the pre- and post-processing moments of design.

Formal Representations in the design space tend to be formal, in the sense of grammar-driven. For example, ER diagrams are constructed using one of many visual grammars. In the area of visualization and communication, representations may follow more or less formal grammars, ranging from the grammar of graphics (Wilkinson 2012; Wickham 2010) to guidelines and principles (Tufte 2006, 1985, 1991).
Non-quantitative Although formal, typically design grammars are not quantitative per se. They focus on structural patterns and relationships that ground quantitative modeling. Indeed, the work of designing data structures in the pre-processing segment of the pipeline lays the groundwork for measurement-based quantitative reasoning in the modeling phases. Similarly, visualizations of the results of statistical models will translate numerically expressed quantities into qualitatively grasped sensory channels, such as color, size, shape, and position.
Logical Design grammars are more or less logical, in the sense of making explicit relationships that may be used to make inferences. For example, the relations (i.e. edges) in an ER diagram define the possible propositions that may be constructed from a data set (which are often expressed in SQL). Less rigidly, the design of an infographic, such as Minard’s map of Napoleon’s Russian campaign, may suggest causal relations by the judicious positioning of graphical elements (Mizono 2017).
Aesthetic In place of deductive reasoning, representations of data in the design space often rely on humans’ aesthetic perception and judgement to convey meaning. (Here, aesthetics refers to sensory perception as well as the perception of emergent properties such as beauty.) The reliance of the human brain on visual information is well known, and representations of data exploit this reliance either implicitly or explicitly through the use of elements such as “contrast, balance, emphasis, movement, white space, proportion, hierarchy, repetition, rhythm, pattern, unity, and variety” (Chapman 2018: terms lower-cased).
Reductive All representations to be effective must be reduced representations — they select and foreground a small set of features from the high-dimensional space of experience. They are not intended to capture all of reality but a salient subset of reality relevant to a context and a task. As Borges wrote in “Funes the Memorius,” “To think is to forget differences, generalize, make abstractions” (Borges 1964: p. 66).
Double-voiced Given their role in mediating between human cognition and computational machinery, representations in this space operate along two axes of meaning — the human and machine. That is, they are often meant to be readable by both humans and machines. This is actually an explicit design goal of SGML (and by descent XML), which was developed to make machine processable text intelligible to humans as well as machines. As Goldfarb, the inventor of SGML says, “XML is a data representation that has the characteristics of a document … [you can] process it as data — by first parsing it to extract the data — or you can present it the way you would a document” (Floyd 1998). To be sure, this is not so true in the case of infographics and data stories. Nevertheless, these representations will always contain elements that are directly traceable to mathematical or algorithmic models.
Playful Finally, the area of design always involves a kind of play, since the formalisms adopted are never the only ones possible for a specific task. By play, we mean that there there is always some choice and interpretation involved in choosing and developing a representation, and that this process is not reducible to a mechanical procedure. In other words, producing effective representations in the design space involves is a combination of science and art.

Data as Representation

A special case of \(H \rightarrow M\) representation is data, as commonly understood by data scientists and data analysts. Although in general usage the word may take on a wide range of meanings, in practice it refers to what is often called “structured information,” where structure typically means tabular structure. The content of such tables is typically populated by discrete numbers and categories. It follows that an essential component of design is the literal creation of data, as a form of representation.
